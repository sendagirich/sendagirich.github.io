English has more sounds than Japanese.

Japanese has only five main vowel sounds that are similar to (but not exactly the same as) vowel sounds in English:

  • æ (あ) cat
  • I (い) in
  • ʊ (う) put
  • e (え) bed
  • ɒ (お) hot

Japanese does not have:

2. Long Vowels
  • iː (as in “see”)
  • ɑː (as in “father”)
  • ɔː (as in “thought”)
  • uː (as in “goose”)
  • ɜː (as in “bird”)
3. Diphthongs
  • eɪ (as in “face”)
  • aɪ (as in “price”)
  • ɔɪ (as in “choice”)
  • aʊ (as in “mouth”)
  • oʊ (as in “goat”)
2. Consonants:
  1. No θ, ð (th sounds)* → Japanese lacks “think” (θ) and “this” (ð).
  2. No /v/* → Often replaced with b (e.g., “violin” → バイオリン baiorin).
  3. No /l/* → The Japanese r ([ɾ]) is used for both English l and ɹ.
  4. No /f/* → Japanese ɸ (ふ) is close but not identical.
  5. No ʃ, ʒ as phonemes* → ʃ appears in し (ɕi) but is palatalized.
  6. No tʃ, dʒ as separate phonemes* → Often realized as affricates before i (e.g., ち tɕi).

Japanese only has five main vowel sounds: a i, u, e, o. although these can be long and short. :noexport

Japanese shares some vowel sounds with English, but even these are not always identical in quality. Here are the closest matches in phonemic script (IPA):

1. Japanese Vowels That (Roughly) Exist in English a (あ) → Similar to English ɑː (as in “father”) but more central [a] rather than back [ɑː]. i (い) → Close to English iː (as in “see”), but shorter and without diphthongization.

  • u (う) → Technically [ɯ̈] (unrounded), but often perceived as English uː (as in “goose”), though very different in articulation.
  • e (え) → Near English eɪ (as in “face”) but without the glide (pure [e̞]).
  • o (お) → Similar to English oʊ (as in “goat”) but without the glide (pure [o̞]).

### Important Notes:

  1. No exact equivalents – Japanese vowels are shorter, purer (monophthongs), and more stable than English vowels.
  2. No tense/lax distinctions – Japanese lacks:
    • ɪ (vs. iː)
    • ɛ (vs. eɪ)
    • ʊ (vs. uː)
  3. No schwa ə – English’s most common vowel doesn’t exist in Japanese.

### Summary of Shared Vowels (IPA):

Japanese Closest English Match Example (English)
a ɑː (but central) “father”
i iː (but shorter) “see”
u uː (but unrounded) “goose”
e eɪ (no glide) “face”
o oʊ (no glide) “goat”

### Key Difference: Japanese vowels are always pronounced clearly, while English vowels often reduce (e.g., schwa ə in “about”) or diphthongize.

Here are the English consonant sounds that standard Japanese lacks, presented in phonemic script (IPA) with examples:

—

### 1. Fricatives (Japanese lacks these entirely)

English Sound (IPA) Example (English) Japanese Approximation
θ (voiceless “th”) “think” Replaced with s (e.g., シンク sinku)
ð (voiced “th”) “this” Replaced with z or d (e.g., ディス disu)
v “very” Replaced with b (e.g., ベリー berii)
ʒ “vision” Replaced with ʑ or dʑ (e.g., ビジョン bidʑon)

### 2. Approximants/Liquids (Japanese lacks distinct phonemes)

English Sound (IPA) Example (English) Japanese Approximation
l “light” Merged with ɾ (e.g., ライト ɾaito)
ɹ (English “r”) “right” Replaced with ɾ (e.g., ライト ɾaito)

### 3. Affricates (Japanese lacks some distinctions)

English Sound (IPA) Example (English) Japanese Approximation
dʒ “judge” Replaced with dʑ (e.g., ジャッジ dʑadʑi)

### 4. Other Missing Consonants

English Sound (IPA) Example (English) Japanese Approximation
f (true labiodental) “foot” Replaced with ɸ (e.g., フット ɸutto)
w (rounded) “water” Replaced with weaker w̜ (e.g., ウォーター w̜oːtaː)

—

### Key Notes:

  1. No voiced/voiceless fricative contrasts (e.g., s vs. z exists, but f vs. v does not).
  2. No l-ɹ distinction → Both merge into Japanese ɾ (a flap).
  3. No θ, ð → These are notoriously difficult for Japanese learners.
  4. h allophony → Japanese h becomes [ç] (before i) or [ɸ] (before u).

—
