in doom-emacs how can I embed a web-link into an image for export to html?

In Doom Emacs with Org mode, use the #+ATTR_HTML attribute above your image link. Example: 

ATTR_HTML: :width 50% :alt Description
file:image.png

On export, this creates an HTML <a> tag wrapping an <img> tag.

1 Pronunciation Key Terms

  • Read through these terms so that you will be able to understand following lessons.

Syllable (音節 おんせつ)

A unit of sound in a word, it usually includes a vowel sound. Examples: “cat” (1 syllable), “water” (2 syllables).

Vowels (母音 ぼいん)

A speech sound. The letters A, E, I, O, U (and sometimes Y) represent vowels. Example sounds: a (as in “cat”), i (as in “see”).

Consonants (子音 しいん)

A speech sound. The letters B, C, D, F, T, S… represent consonants. Examples in English: B (as in “bat”), S (as in “sun”), M (as in “man”).

Voiced consonants (声の子音)

A voiced consonant is a consonant that is produced with the vibration of the vocal cords. For example:

  • [z] as in zip is a voiced consonant
  • [s] as in sip an unvoiced consonant.

🔗 Voiced consonants

Unvoiced (voiceless) consonants (声のない子音)

A type of consonant sound that is produced without the vibration of the vocal cords. 🔗 Unvoiced consonants

Word stress (強勢 / きょうせい)

Emphasis on syllables within words (e.g., REcord vs. reCORD).

Sentence stress (文強勢 ぶんきょうせい)

Emphasis on words in a sentence. (e.g., I went to school on Thursday.)

Intonation (抑揚 / よくよう)

Pitch variation in speech.

Rhythm (リズム / りずむ)

Pattern of stressed/unstressed syllables.

Weak form (Schwa) / /ə / (弱形 / シュワー音)

Neutral vowel in unstressed syllables (e.g., “sofa”).

Consonant clusters

  • Consonants often come next to each other in English (pl, cl, tr, shl…), unlike Japanese that has consonant-vowel.

  • Remember not to add a vowel sound between consonants if they should not be there.

    fly not fulai fry not furai play not pulay bring not buringu

  • Look back at the Consonant blends section of the Syllables lesson if you need to practise.

4 Rhythm

  • How the rhythm of Spoken English is different from spoken Japanese.
  • How English rhythm influences what sounds we hear and say.
  • The rhythm of spoken English is different from Japanese and many other languages.
  • Understanding the basic rhythm of English will help you to understand why spoken English sounds so different from Japanese and will help you become a better speaker and listener.

Transcript

  • Today’s mini English lesson is about English being a stress time language.
  • All languages are on a scale from being stress timed or syllable timed.
  • English is strongly stress timed and Spanish (and Japanese!) is strongly syllable timed.
  • This means that you don’t pronounce every syllable but for example it’s Spanish you do:
  • En esapnol pronunciamos cada syllaba. (Spanish)
  • But in English we only stress the important words:
    • 1 2 3 4
    • a 1 a 2 a 3 a 4
    • a 1 then a 2 then a 3 then a 4
  • The number of words is increased but the beat is the same.
  • Music and poetry demonstrate this really well:
    • We all live in a yellow submarine, yellow submarine, yellow submarine.
  • So important words like nouns and some verbs are stressed and less important words are unstressed.
  • In this form they’re called weak forms.
  • Weak forms can be auxiliaries
    • Does becomes / dəz /
      • Where dəz he live
    • can becomes / kən /
      • What kən I do for you?
  • Personal pronouns
    • Your becomes jə
      • What does jə boss think?
  • Prepositions
    • Of becomes / əv /
      • That’s the last əv the crisps
  • Conjunctions
    • Then because / ðən /
      • She’s older ðən you.
  • Articles
    • A becomes ə
      • She’s a teacher
  • As you can see rhythm of English is very different from French or Spanish (and Japanese!) which has syllable timed languages.
  • I hope you’ve enjoyed this video.

Key points for presentations

  • Stressing the important words will make your presentations more interesting for your audience as well as making them easier to understand.
  • If you write everything in katakana it will be very difficult to use word stress and good rhythm!

7 Sentence stress

  • How we stress different words in a sentence.

Intro

Spoken Japanese does not use sentence stress. It is therefore important to listen to lots of English and practice using sentence stress. Using good sentence stress will make your English easier to understand.

The human voice: It’s the instrument we all play. It’s the most powerful sound in the world, probably. It’s the only one that can start a war or say “I love you.” And yet many people have the experience that when they speak, people don’t listen to them.

And why is that? How can we speak powerfully to make change in the world? What I’d like to suggest, there are a number of habits that we need to move away from.

I’ve assembled for your pleasure here seven deadly sins of speaking. I’m not pretending this is an exhaustive list, but these seven, I think, are pretty large habits that we can all fall into.

First, gossip. Speaking ill of somebody who’s not present. Not a nice habit, and we know perfectly well the person gossiping, five minutes later, will be gossiping about us.

Second, judging. We know people who are like this in conversation, and it’s very hard to listen to somebody if you know that you’re being judged and found wanting at the same time.

Third, negativity. You can fall into this. My mother, in the last years of her life, became very negative, and it’s hard to listen. I remember one day, I said to her, “It’s October 1 today,” and she said, “I know, isn’t it dreadful?” (Laughter) It’s hard to listen when somebody’s that negative. (Laughter)

in doom-emacs how can I embed a web-link into an image for export to html?

In Doom Emacs with Org mode, use the #+ATTR_HTML attribute above your image link. Example: 

ATTR_HTML: :width 50% :alt Description
file:image.png

On export, this creates an HTML <a> tag wrapping an <img> tag.

ATTR_HTML: :width 50% :alt Description

The Org-mode syntax you’re using is incorrect for creating a clickable image link. Here’s the proper way: 

file:https://sendagirich.github.io/asuka/assets/shots/shotsizes.jpg

Or if you want the image itself to be clickable: 

https://sendagirich.github.io/asuka/assets/shots/shotsizes.jpg

But for an image that’s clickable, you need HTML export attributes: 

ATTR_HTML: :target _blank
https://sendagirich.github.io/asuka/assets/shots/shotsizes.jpg

The issue with your original code is that you have two URLs nested in a single link, which Org-mode interprets as a regular text link rather than an image link.
