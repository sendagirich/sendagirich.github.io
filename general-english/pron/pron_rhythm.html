Rhythm

The rhythm of spoken English is different from Japanese and many other languages.

Understanding the basic rhythm of English will help you to understand why spoken English sounds so different from Japanese and will help you become a better speaker and listener.

Transcript

  • Today’s mini English lesson is about English being a stress time language.
  • All languages are on a scale from being stress timed or syllable timed.
  • English is strongly stress timed and Spanish (and Japanese!) is strongly syllable timed.
  • This means that you don’t pronounce every syllable but for example it’s Spanish you do:
  • En esapnol pronunciamos cada syllaba. (Spanish)
  • But in English we only stress the important words:
    • 1 2 3 4
    • a 1 a 2 a 3 a 4
    • a 1 then a 2 then a 3 then a 4
  • The number of words is increased but the beat is the same.
  • Music and poetry demonstrate this really well:
    • We all live in a yellow submarine, yellow submarine, yellow submarine.
  • So important words like nouns and some verbs are stressed and less important words are unstressed.
  • In this form they’re called weak forms.
  • Weak forms can be auxiliaries
    • Does becomes / dəz /
      • Where dəz he live
    • can becomes / kən /
      • What kən I do for you?
  • Personal pronouns
    • Your becomes jə
      • What does jə boss think?
  • Prepositions
    • Of becomes / əv /
      • That’s the last əv the crisps
  • Conjunctions
    • Then because / ðən /
      • She’s older ðən you.
  • Articles
    • A becomes ə
      • She’s a teacher
  • As you can see rhythm of English is very different from French or Spanish (and Japanese!) which has syllable timed languages.
  • I hope you’ve enjoyed this video.

Key points for presentations

Stressing the important words will make your presentations more interesting for your audience as well as making them easier to understand.

If you write everything in katakana it will be very difficult to use word stress and good rhythm!
