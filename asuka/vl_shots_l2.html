Camera movement

  • Shots where the camera moves are called travelling shots.
  • Shots where the camera does not move are called static shots.

Static shots

  • We learned about these shots last lesson.
    • Close-Up (CU) – Face or object fills the frame; highlights emotion or detail.
    • Extreme Close-Up (ECU) – Isolates a small detail (e.g., eyes, lips).
    • Two-Shot – Frames two subjects together.
    • Over-the-Shoulder (OTS) – Shot from behind one subject, focusing on another.
    • Establishing Shot – Sets the scene/location at the start.
    • Medium Shot (MS) – Waist up; emphasizes body language.
  • Here some more common shots:
    • Extreme Wide Shot (EWS) – Shows vast surroundings; subject is small or not visible.
    • Wide Shot (WS) – Frames the subject fully within their environment.
    • Full Shot (FS) – Captures the subject head-to-toe.
    • Medium Wide Shot (MWS) / Cowboy Shot – Frames from mid-thigh up.
    • Medium Close-Up (MCU) – Chest/shoulders up; focuses on facial expressions.
    • Point-of-View (POV) – Represents what a character sees.
  • Shots can be a combination of different shots.
    • For example an establishing shot is also either a static or travelling shot and is also often a wide shot.

Travelling shots

  • The camera can move in different ways in travelling shots.
  • The camera can:
    • zoom in or out.
      • The camera does not move but the lens does.
    • tilt up or down.
    • pan left or right.
    • dolly in or out.
      • The camera moves to follow the action.
  • The camera does not move but the lens does.
    1. Zoom shot – Lens adjusts focal length.
    2. Tilt – Vertical pivot on a fixed spot.
    3. Pan – Horizontal pivot on a fixed spot.
  • We can use these as verbs:
    • zoom in or out.
    • tilt up or down.
    • pan left or right.
  • The camera moves to follow the action.
    1. Dolly shot – Camera moves smoothly on tracks.
    2. Tracking shot – Follows a subject horizontally.
      • dolly in or out.
      • track left or right
      • track the main character
    3. Steadicam shot – Smooth handheld movement.
    4. Handheld shot – Raw, shaky movement.

Form and meaning

  • In painting, photography and films form e.g. composition, colour, size show meaning.
  • In film, the choice of shot is an important way to help tell a story.
  • For example, a wide long shot can show that a character is lonely. A quick zoom in to an extreme close up can show shock or excitement.
  • Here are some phrases to describe how shots help to tell the story.
  • Make your own sentences.
